\section{Background and preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Manifold-supported observations and notation}
\label{sec:prelim:notation}

Under the manifold hypothesis, high-dimensional observations are concentrated on or near a smooth low-dimensional manifold embedded in ambient space \cite{Cayton_2008,Whiteley_2025}. This is the working assumption behind most modern representation learning and latent-variable generative modelling approaches [TODO: CITE]. Dimension reduction is an easily-motivated goal in this setting: a \(d\)-dimensional support in \(\mathbb{R}^D\) with \(d<D\) is singular with respect to \(D\)-dimensional Lebesgue measure, so a more natural object to model is the intrinsic density on the manifold. As well as this, working in a lower-dimensional latent space also mitigates the curse of dimensionality in downstream procedures (such as Monte-Carlo-based techniques) and provides an inductive bias toward regularisation and denoising [CITE]. We therefore describe data
\[
X \in \cM \subset \mathbb{R}^D,
\qquad
\dim(\cM) = m < D,
\qquad
\mathbb{P}(X \in \cM) = 1,
\]
with \(\cM\) a smooth, noncompact, embedded manifold.

Without loss of generality, we work throughout with optionally-recentred coordinates: the raw observation may be trivially replaced by \(X-c\) for a fixed or learnable \(c\in\mathbb{R}^D\), and \(X-c\) is treated as the generic \(\mathbb{O}_D\)-valued random vector to which all subsequent definitions and theorems apply. This is a preprocessing convention rather than a feature of the model. From here on, \(X\) denotes the recentred random vector.

Regular variation, the second ingredient of our setting (described in more depth in \S\ref{sec:prelim:rv}), concerns behaviour at infinity and is defined on the \emph{punctured} ambient and latent spaces
\[
\mathbb{O}_D := \mathbb{R}^D \setminus \{0\},
\qquad
\mathbb{O}_m := \mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{0\},
\]
and assume \(\mathbb{P}(X=0)=0\) so that \(X\) is almost surely \(\mathbb{O}_D\)-valued.

As well as this, the unit \(q\)-spheres of \(\mathbb{R}^D\) and \(\mathbb{R}^m\) for a norm exponent \(q\in[1,\infty]\) are defined as
\[
\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q} := \{u \in \mathbb{R}^D : \|u\|_q = 1\},
\qquad
\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q} := \{v \in \mathbb{R}^m : \|v\|_q = 1\},
\]
and each \(x\in\mathbb{O}_D\) admits the unique radial--angular decomposition \(x = \|x\|_q\,(x/\|x\|_q)\). The exponent \(q\) is kept general throughout, however a reader may trivially specialise to \(q{=}2\), as we do in all experiments.

Our aim is to learn a latent representation of \(X\) in a lower-dimensional Euclidean\footnote{In this work, the data manifold is assumed to be diffeomorphic to \(\RR^m\), so that a single Euclidean latent chart is topologically admissible. Extending the presented framework to manifolds requiring multiple charts or nontrivial latent topology would be a natural direction for further research.} space that preserves its tail asymptotics. To that end we introduce an encoder
\[f:\mathbb{O}_D\to\mathbb{O}_m\] and a decoder \[h:\mathbb{O}_m\to\mathbb{O}_D\], and write
\[
Z := f(X)
\]
for the latent representation of \(X\).

\subsection{Regular variation}
\label{sec:prelim:rv}

Heavy-tailed phenomena are commonly encountered in topics such as meteorology, finance and astronomy, where extreme events dominate practical concerns yet are only rarely observed \cite{resnick_heavy-tail_2007,DeHaan_2006}. A way of characterising such heavy-tailed processes is via the framework of regular variation. We say that the \(\mathbb{O}_D\)-valued random vector \(X\) is \emph{regularly varying} with index \(\alpha>0\) if there exist a scaling function \(b:(0,\infty)\to(0,\infty)\) with \(b(t)\to\infty\) and a nonzero measure \(\mu\) on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) such that
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)}\in\cdot\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
\mu(\cdot)
\qquad\text{in } \mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D);
\]
the limit \(\mu\) is the \emph{ambient tail measure} of \(X\) and is \(\alpha\)-homogeneous, \(\mu(\lambda A)=\lambda^{-\alpha}\mu(A)\) for every \(\lambda>0\) \cite{lindskog_regularly_2014}. A more-in depth description of regular variation and measure-homogeneity is given in \Cref{sec:appendix:metrics:rv}.

The manifold-support assumption is geometric (where \(X\) lives) while regular variation is asymptotic, the two constraints are independent, and our framework respects both.

\subsection{Positively homogeneous maps}
\label{sec:prelim:homogeneous}

\begin{definition}[Positive homogeneity]
A measurable map \(f\) between Euclidean spaces is called \emph{positively homogeneous of degree \(p>0\)}~\todo{CITE \S1.11} if 
% ~\todo{CITE Euler 1755 / Widder 1961 / Aliprantis \& Border 2006 \S1.11} 
\[
f(\lambda x)=\lambda^{p} f(x),
\qquad
\lambda>0.
\]
\end{definition}

We treat \(p>0\) as a design parameter of the model (\S\ref{sec:encoder-architecture}). Unless stated otherwise, \(p=1\) in all experiments. It is also worth noting the distinction between \emph{map-homogeneity} \(f(\lambda x)=\lambda^p f(x)\) and \emph{measure-homogeneity} \(\mu(\lambda A)=\lambda^{-\alpha}\mu(A)\).

Two standard facts about positively homogeneous maps and about tail measures under measurable transformations drive the construction in \S\ref{sec:method}. The first gives a clean parameterisation of positively homogeneous maps by their restriction to the unit sphere; the second describes how tail measures transport through measurable maps.

\begin{proposition}[Radial--angular factorisation of positively homogeneous maps]
\label{prop:canonical-homogeneous-form}
Fix \(q\in[1,\infty]\) and \(p\in\mathbb{R}\). A map \(f:\mathbb{O}_D\to\mathbb{R}^m\) is positively homogeneous of degree \(p\) if and only if there exists a unique map \(g:\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_q\to\mathbb{R}^m\) such that
\[
f(x) = \|x\|_q^{\,p}\, g\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_q}\right),
\qquad x\in\mathbb{O}_D.
\]
\end{proposition}

\noindent Proof in \Cref{sec:appendix:canonical-form}. The encoder architecture of \S\ref{sec:encoder-architecture} parameterises the angular map \(g\) directly, which enforces \(p\)-homogeneity by construction.

\begin{theorem}[Mapping theorem {\cite[Theorem~2.3]{lindskog_regularly_2014}}]
\label{thm:mapping-theorem}
Let \(\varphi:\mathbb{O}\to\mathbb{O}'\) be measurable, and assume \(\varphi^{-1}(A')\) is bounded away from the origin for every measurable \(A'\subset\mathbb{O}'\) bounded away from the origin of \(\mathbb{O}'\). Then the pushforward map \(\widehat\varphi:\nu\mapsto\nu\circ\varphi^{-1}\) from \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O})\) to \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}')\) is continuous at any \(\mu\) with \(\mu(D_\varphi)=0\), where \(D_\varphi\) denotes the discontinuity set of \(\varphi\).~\todo{also cite Billingsley 1968/1999 Thm.~5.5}
\end{theorem}

\noindent Together, \Cref{prop:canonical-homogeneous-form,thm:mapping-theorem} yield the encoder tail-transport identity \Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders} in closed form; see \Cref{sec:appendix:metrics} for extended discussion.

\subsection{Learning goal}
\label{sec:prelim:goal}

Given observations \(X \in \cM \subset \mathbb{R}^D\) with
\(\dim(\cM)=m<D\), \(\mathbb{P}(X\in\cM)=1\), and regularly varying law
with tail measure \(\mu_X\), our principal aim is to learn measurable maps
\((f,h)\) such that \emph{(i)} the latent representation
\(Z:=f(X)\) is regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) with tail measure
\(f_{\#}\mu_X=\mu_X\circ f^{-1}\), and \emph{(ii)} the reconstruction
\(h(Z)\) is regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) with the original ambient
tail index \(\alpha\) and with a tail measure computable as the homogeneous
pushforward
\[
(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X.
\]
Thus the framework guarantees preservation of the radial tail index and
provides an explicit description of the reconstructed tail measure. Exact
recovery of the original ambient tail measure \(\mu_X\) would require the
additional asymptotic inverse condition
\[
(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X=\mu_X,
\]
which is not assumed in this theory but is an interesting avenue for future work \todo{expand on this/work out what this means}.

\section{Method: Homogeneous Autoencoders}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Design principles}
\label{sec:design-principles}

We now specify the proposed autoencoder architecture. The design separates the
roles of encoding and decoding: the encoder is made exactly \(p\)-homogeneous,
so that, by \Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}, the ambient
tail measure is transported to latent space as the pushforward through the
learnt map; the decoder is constructed to be asymptotically homogeneous,
providing the expressivity required to reconstruct a curved manifold while still
being tail-equivalent to a homogeneous map by
\Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence}. Importantly, a \emph{globally}
homogeneous decoder can represent only conic image geometries
(\Cref{rmk:globally-homogeneous-decoder}), which is too restrictive for a
generic curved \(\cM\). Flexibility of the decoder is enabled at finite scales
through a radius-dependent angular correction whose magnitude goes to zero at
infinity by construction, rather than by a soft loss-based mechanism. The
architecture is summarised by three key properties:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The encoder is exactly \(p\)-homogeneous by construction
  (\S\ref{sec:encoder-architecture}).
  \item The decoder's leading term \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\) is positively
  homogeneous of degree \(1/p\), inverting the encoder's radial scaling
  (\S\ref{sec:decoder-architecture}).
  \item The decoder's angular correction \(\delta(\eta,\rho)\) satisfies
  \(\sup_\eta \|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_q\to0\) as \(\rho\to\infty\), enforced by a
  compact-domain reparametrisation
  (\S\ref{sec:decoder-architecture}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Encoder architecture}
\label{sec:encoder-architecture}

By \Cref{prop:canonical-homogeneous-form}, every positively homogeneous map \(f:\mathbb{O}_D\to\mathbb{R}^m\) of degree \(p\) is determined by its restriction to the unit \(q\)-sphere via
\[
f(x)=\|x\|_{q}^p\,g\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right),
\qquad
g:\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q}\to\mathbb{R}^m,
\qquad x\in\mathbb{O}_D,
\]
so, in order to construct a homogeneous encoder, it is sufficient to parameterise the angular map \(g\). Wherever \(g(\theta)\neq 0\) we \(g\) can be further decomposed into magnitude and direction:
\[
g(\theta)=a(\theta)\,e(\theta),
\qquad
a(\theta):=\|g(\theta)\|_{q},
\qquad
e(\theta):=\frac{g(\theta)}{\|g(\theta)\|_{q}}\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q},
\]
giving the encoder form as
\[
f(x)
=
\|x\|_{q}^p\;
a\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right)\,
e\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right),
\]
in which \(\|x\|_{q}^p\) is the homogeneous radial term, \(a\) is an angle-dependent scaling, and \(e\) is an angular map from the ambient sphere into the latent sphere. The \(a\)--\(e\) split plays no role in the tail-transport theory that follows. It is retained only because it provides an analogous construction for the decoder as well as being useful for training-time diagnostics, architectural regularisation and latent-space visualisation.

\begin{proposition}[Regular variation under homogeneous encoders]
\label{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}
Let \(X\) be regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) with scaling function \(b\), index \(\alpha>0\), and tail measure \(\mu\), i.e.
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)} \in \cdot \right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
\mu(\cdot)
\qquad
\text{in } \mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D).
\]
Let
\[
f:\mathbb{O}_D \to \mathbb{O}_m
\]
be measurable and suppose:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \(f\) is continuous \(\mu\)-almost everywhere;
    \item for every measurable \(A \subset \mathbb{O}_m\) bounded away from \(0\), the preimage \(f^{-1}(A)\) is bounded away from \(0\) in \(\mathbb{O}_D\);
    \item \(f\) is positively homogeneous of degree \(p>0\), i.e.
    \[
    f(\lambda x)=\lambda^p f(x),
    \qquad \lambda>0.
    \]
\end{enumerate}
Then \(f(X)\) is regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) with scaling function \(b_f(t)=b(t)^p\) and tail measure
\[
\mu_f = f_{\#}\mu = \mu\circ f^{-1}.
\]
Moreover, \(\mu_f\) is \(\frac{\alpha}{p}\)-homogeneous:
\[
\mu_f(\lambda A)=\lambda^{-\frac{\alpha}{p}}\mu_f(A),
\qquad
\lambda>0.
\]
Equivalently, \(f(X)\) is regularly varying with tail index \(\frac{\alpha}{p}\).
\end{proposition}

Proof available in
\Cref{sec:appendix:proof-encoder-tail-transport}.


\begin{remark}[Choice of \(p\)]
\label{rmk:choice-of-p}
The degree \(p>0\) is a chosen design parameter: an ambient tail index \(\alpha\) becomes a latent tail index \(\alpha/p\), so \(p\)  rescales the heaviness of the distribution in latent space. Unless stated otherwise we use \(p=1\), so the latent and ambient indices agree. A practitioner may prefer \(p\neq 1\) to match a prescribed latent heaviness or to equalise differing tail indices across multimodal encoders. Very small \(p\) compresses the latent radial dynamic range while very large \(p\) amplifies radial detail.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Decoder architecture}
\label{sec:decoder-architecture}

\todo{generate some diagrams for this?}

\begin{remark}[Why the decoder should not be globally homogeneous]
\label{rmk:globally-homogeneous-decoder}
Let \(h:\mathbb{O}_m\to\mathbb{O}_D\) be positively homogeneous of any positive degree \(\gamma\). If \(x=h(z)\) then for every \(\lambda>0\), \(\lambda x = h(\lambda^{1/\gamma} z)\), so \(h(\mathbb{O}_m)\) is invariant under positive dilations and hence conic about the origin. A globally homogeneous decoder can therefore represent only conic images, which is too restrictive for a generic manifold with unknown curvature.
\end{remark}

We therefore construct the decoder with a homogeneous leading term plus a radius-dependent angular correction designed to vanish in the tail. Writing the latent variable in polar form,
\[
z=\rho\,\eta,
\qquad
\rho:=\|z\|_{q},
\qquad
\eta:=\frac{z}{\|z\|_{q}}\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q},
\]
and recalling that the encoder scales ambient radius by the power \(p\), we invert this scaling and formulate the homogeneous term as
\[
h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)
=
\rho^{1/p}\,\tilde a(\eta)\,\tilde e(\eta),
\qquad
\tilde e:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}\to \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q},
\qquad
\tilde a:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}\to (0,\infty),
\]
which can be understood as the inverse analogue of the encoder parameterisation \(f(x)=\|x\|_{q}^{p}\,a(\theta)\,e(\theta)\). To allow the decoder to reconstruct finite-scale curvature, we introduce a correction term
\[
\delta:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}\times(0,\infty)\to \mathbb{R}^D
\]
which is a function of the latent direction \(\eta\) and latent radius \(\rho=\|z\|_{q}\), and define the corrected decoded direction
\[
\tilde c(\eta,\rho)
:=
\frac{\tilde e(\eta)+\delta(\eta,\rho)}
{\bigl\|\tilde e(\eta)+\delta(\eta,\rho)\bigr\|_{q}}
\in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q},
\qquad
\bigl\|\tilde e(\eta)+\delta(\eta,\rho)\bigr\|_{q}\neq 0.
\]
The full decoder is then
\[
h(z)
=
\rho^{1/p}\,\tilde a(\eta)\,\tilde c(\eta,\rho),
\qquad
z\in\mathbb{O}_{m};
\]
if \(\delta\equiv 0\) then \(\tilde c\equiv\tilde e\), and \(h\) reduces to the positively homogeneous map \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\) of degree \(1/p\).

Asymptotic homogeneity of \(h\) is delivered by an architectural trick. Transform the latent radius via
\[
s(\rho):=\frac{1}{1+\rho}\in(0,1],
\]
so that \(\rho\to\infty\) becomes the boundary point \(s=0\), and formulate \(\delta\) by subtracting the value at that boundary:

Let \(\Delta:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}\times[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}^D\) be continuous, and define
\[
\delta(\eta,\rho):=\Delta\bigl(\eta,s(\rho)\bigr)-\Delta(\eta,0).
\]
Continuity of \(\Delta\) on the compact set \(\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}\times[0,1]\) then forces \(\sup_\eta\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|\to 0\) as \(\rho\to\infty\) by the following lemma.

\begin{lemma}[Uniform convergence of the corrected decoder]
\label{lem:uniform-angular-convergence}
Let \(\Delta\), \(\delta\), and \(\tilde c\) be as above and assume
\[
\inf_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q},\,\rho\in(0,\infty)}
\bigl\|\tilde e(\eta)+\delta(\eta,\rho)\bigr\|_{q}>0.
\]
Then
\[
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}}\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_q\to 0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}}
\bigl\|\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\bigr\|_q\to 0
\qquad\text{as }\rho\to\infty.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Heine--Cantor theorem \todo{CITE?} on the compact domain \(\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q}\times[0,1]\) for the first limit, then the assumption keeps the denominator of \(\tilde c\) away from zero so that the limit passes to the full \(\tilde c\) term. Full proof in \Cref{sec:appendix:proofs}.
\end{proof}

% The requirement that the convergence be \emph{uniform} in \(\eta\) is what lets \Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence} transport the bound onto regularly varying sequences; pointwise convergence would not suffice. The nondegeneracy condition \textnormal{(ND)} is mild --- it holds automatically whenever \(\sup_{\eta,\rho}\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_{q}<1\), since \(\|\tilde e(\eta)\|_{q}=1\) --- and is readily checked at training time. Consequently, the decoder is asymptotically homogeneous while retaining expressiveness at finite scales through variation of \(\Delta(\eta,s)\) over \(s\in(0,1]\); density of such architectures in the space of continuous angular corrections is discussed in \Cref{sec:appendix:extra-theory}.

\begin{definition}[Tail equivalence]
\label{def:tail-equivalence}
Let \(Y\) and \(\widetilde Y\) be \(\mathbb{O}_D\)-valued random vectors.
We say that \(Y\) and \(\widetilde Y\) are \emph{tail-equivalent} with
scaling function \(b\) and tail measure \(\nu\) if
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{Y}{b(t)}\in\cdot\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;\nu(\cdot),
\qquad
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{\widetilde Y}{b(t)}\in\cdot\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;\nu(\cdot)
\]
in \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D)\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Tail equivalence under asymptotically homogeneous decoding]
\label{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence}
Let \(Z\) be regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) with scaling function
\(b_Z\), tail index \(\beta>0\), and tail measure \(\mu_Z\). Write
\[
z=\rho\eta,
\qquad
\rho:=\|z\|_q,
\qquad
\eta:=z/\|z\|_q\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q.
\]
Define
\[
h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)
=
\rho^{1/p}\,\tilde a(\eta)\,\tilde e(\eta),
\qquad
h(z)
=
\rho^{1/p}\,\tilde a(\eta)\,\tilde c(\eta,\rho),
\]
where
\[
\tilde a:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q\to(0,\infty),
\qquad
\tilde e:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q\to\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_q.
\]
Assume:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(A1)] \(\tilde a\) is continuous and bounded above and below:
  \[
  0<a_{\min}\le \tilde a(\eta)\le a_{\max}<\infty
  \qquad
  \text{for all }\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q;
  \]
  \item[(A2)] \(\tilde e\) is continuous;
  \item[(A3)] \(h\) is well-defined on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) and the corrected
  angular term converges uniformly to the homogeneous angular term:
  \[
  \varepsilon(\rho)
  :=
  \sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q}
  \|\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\|_q
  \xrightarrow[\rho\to\infty]{}0.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
Then \(h(Z)\) and \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\) are tail-equivalent on
\(\mathbb{O}_D\) with scaling function \(b_Z(t)^{1/p}\), common tail index
\(p\beta\), and common tail measure
\[
(h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(
\frac{h(Z)}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}\in\cdot
\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
(h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z(\cdot)
\qquad
\text{in }\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D).
\]
\end{theorem}

\noindent\emph{Proof sketch.}
The homogeneous map \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\) is continuous, positively homogeneous
of degree \(1/p\), and maps sets bounded away from the origin to preimages
bounded away from the origin because \(a_{\min}\le\tilde a\le a_{\max}\).
The mapping theorem therefore gives regular variation of \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\)
with scaling \(b_Z^{1/p}\) and tail measure \((h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z\).
The perturbation satisfies
\[
\|h(z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)\|_q
\le
a_{\max}\rho^{1/p}\varepsilon(\rho),
\qquad
\varepsilon(\rho)\to0.
\]
A converging-together argument in \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D)\) shows that this
perturbation is negligible after normalisation by \(b_Z(t)^{1/p}\), hence \(h(Z)\)
has the same tail limit as \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\). Full proof in
\Cref{sec:appendix:proof-decoder-tail-equivalence}.

\subsection{Training objective}
\label{sec:training-objective}

In practice, the angular maps \(a,\,e,\,\tilde a,\,\tilde e\) and the correction term \(\Delta\) are constructed as MLPs \todo{define?}, with \(a,\,\tilde a\) enforced positive via softplus \todo{define?} and directional \(e,\,\tilde e\) \(q\)-normalised onto the appropriate sphere \todo{could discuss spherical neural networks etc but this still works}. Full specifications are given in \Cref{sec:appendix:architecture}. We train the encoder and decoder jointly to minimise reconstruction error while penalising deviations from homogeneous decoding more heavily at larger ambient radii. Fix a norm \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{pen}}\) on \(\mathbb{R}^D\) and a nondecreasing, unbounded weight function \(\omega:(0,\infty)\to(0,\infty)\). Writing \(\hat X = h(f(X))\), \(r\) for the ambient norm of the input, and \(Z=f(X)=\rho\eta\), the per-sample objective is
\[
\mathcal{L}(X)
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}(X,\hat X)
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}\,
\omega(r)\,
\bigl\|
\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)
\bigr\|_{\mathrm{pen}}.
\]
The first term is a generic reconstruction loss which may be freely chosen by the practitioner. We use mean-squared error (MSE) for simplicity, but the framework is agnostic to this choice. Other settings will likely require a more tailored approach to setting the loss function: a spectral loss for physical extremes \todo{cite these bits} or a likelihood term for probabilistic data are equally compatible, since the tail-preservation guarantee of \Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence} comes from the architecture rather than a specific loss formulation. The second term penalises the magnitude of the angular correction, with \(\omega\) sharpening the penalty toward the tail so that the decoder uses the non-homogeneous mechanism only where it materially improves reconstruction. In our implementation, we choose
\[
\omega(r)=\log(1+r).
\]
it grows slowly enough not to dominate gradient signal from the reconstruction term at moderate radii, while still assigning signficant cost to departures from the homogeneous decoder in the tails. In our implementation, the correction penalty weight \(\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}\) is ratcheted adaptively during training. For full specifications of this, see \Cref{sec:appendix:training}.

It is important to emphasise that the asymptotic limit in \Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence} is enforced analytically by the construction of \(\delta\), not by the loss. The role of the radius-weighted penalty is optimisation-based: it discourages unnecessary finite-sample use of the correction in high-radius regions and makes the empirical transition to the homogeneous regime occur earlier in radius.


\subsection{Round-trip tail preservation}
\label{sec:theory}

Combining encoder tail transport (\Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}) with decoder tail equivalence (\Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence}) gives the round-trip guarantee.

\begin{corollary}[Tail-index preservation under the round trip]
\label{cor:tail-index-preservation}
Let \(X\) be regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) with scaling function \(b_X\), tail index \(\alpha>0\), and tail measure \(\mu_X\). Let \(f\) be a \(p\)-homogeneous encoder satisfying the hypotheses of \Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}, and \(h\) an asymptotically homogeneous decoder satisfying \Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence} with exponent \(1/p\). Then \(h(f(X))\) is regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) with scaling function \(b_X\), tail index \(\alpha\), and tail measure \((h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X\); the radial index propagates as
\(\alpha\xrightarrow{f}\alpha/p\xrightarrow{h}\alpha\).
\end{corollary}

The proof is in \Cref{sec:appendix:proof-tail-index-preservation}. The corollary preserves the radial tail index and gives an explicit reconstructed tail measure, but does not on its own imply \((h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X=\mu_X\): full angular tail-measure recovery is an additional asymptotic inverse condition that the architecture does not enforce. Latent-space extreme-value analysis is therefore calibrated at the level of the global radial tail index by construction, while applications requiring exact angular recovery must impose or empirically verify the stronger condition (further discussion in \Cref{sec:appendix:index-vs-measure}).

\subsection{Real data: ERA5 surface fields}
\label{sec:experiments:climate}

We test whether the tail-preservation seen on synthetic data
carries to real reanalysis data, where no ground-truth tail index
is available. We use ERA5 \citep{Hersbach_2020} on an arbitrarily
chosen regional patch over southwest England and Wales
$[53.5^\circ\mathrm{N},\,5.5^\circ\mathrm{W};\,50^\circ\mathrm{N},\,2.5^\circ\mathrm{W}]$
at $0.25^\circ$ native resolution (ambient dimension $D{=}195$ grid
cells per variable), 6-hourly over 1979--2023 ($\approx 66\mathrm{k}$
samples), with a deterministic train/val/test split. We run three univariate
experiments ($u_{10}$ surface wind, $t_{2m}$ 2\,m temperature, $tp$
total precipitation, latent $m{=}8$ for $u_{10},t_{2m}$ and $m{=}32$
for $tp$) and one \emph{joint} experiment that stacks all five
surface variables into a single ambient tensor of dimension $D{=}975$
compressed to a $32$-dimensional latent code. Architectures are
parameter-matched as in \S\ref{sec:experiments:synthetic}; PCA is
reported as a linear reference at the same latent dimension.

The pattern seen on the headline benchmark persists across all four
runs (Table~\ref{tab:climate}): the HomogeneousAE preserves the
ambient tail index by one to seven orders of magnitude relative to
the parameter-matched StandardAE, while matching its bulk
reconstruction MSE and beating PCA on every variable. The joint
multivariate run is the most striking case --- a $32$-dimensional
embedding of a $975$-dimensional field with latent Hill drift
indistinguishable from zero, against a StandardAE whose latent tail
estimator has drifted by $\approx 1$ (the latent code no longer
looks heavy-tailed at all). This is the practical consequence of
homogeneity-by-construction: at matched reconstruction the latent
codes transport tail structure with no per-variable tuning.
Per-variable Hill plots and spatial reconstruction-error maps are
in \Cref{sec:appendix:climate}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{When does it work?}
\todopar{D-1}{The method works best when the ambient manifold is
asymptotically conical -- which is implied by regular variation on the
tail measure's support. For manifolds with persistent nontrivial
curvature at all scales, the asymptotic-homogeneity bias can cost
reconstruction accuracy in the tail. This is \emph{observable}: when
the correction norm $\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|$ does not decay as
$\rho\to\infty$, the model is being forced onto a cone the data does
not lie on, and this should be treated as a diagnostic signal rather
than as quiet mis-specification.

\textbf{Prose prompts:}
(1) Is ``asymptotically conical'' defined in the body glossary or forward-referenced -- a reader seeing the phrase for the first time in \S7 may not know it means ``for every $\epsilon$, the manifold lies in an $\epsilon$-tube around a cone outside a compact set''?
(2) Can the ``observable'' clause be turned into a checkable procedure: ``plot $\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|$ vs $\rho$; if it does not decay, the architecture's assumption is violated for your data''?
(3) Does the paragraph name the \emph{sufficient} condition (conical support of the tail measure) \emph{and} the necessary failure mode (persistent curvature) so the reader knows when to trust HAE and when to reach for a different tool?}

\paragraph{Hyperparameter guidance.}
\todopar{D-2}{$p$ is the homogeneity degree of the encoder (default
$p{=}1$); $\alpha$ is the ambient tail index, auto-estimated via the
Hill estimator implemented in
\texttt{experiments/lib/metrics.py:hill\_estimate}. The correction
weight $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$ is ratcheted adaptively
(\Cref{sec:appendix:training}); a useful default is the two-phase
schedule with warmup patience $N_{\mathrm{recon}}{=}100$ on the
validation reconstruction loss (safety cap
$T_{\mathrm{warmup-max}}{=}1000$), growth $g{=}1.10$, decay $d{=}0.95$,
tolerance $\tau{=}0.5$, and $\lambda_{\max}{=}1$.

\textbf{Prose prompts:}
(1) For each of $p$, $\alpha$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$: is there a one-line \emph{diagnostic} telling the practitioner whether the current value is too small or too large (Hill drift not flat $\Rightarrow$ $p$ wrong; correction norm not decaying $\Rightarrow$ $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$ too small; etc.)?
(2) Does the paragraph tell the reader which hyperparameters they ever need to touch ($\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$ schedule in heavy-curvature regimes; $p$ if they want a specific latent $\alpha/p$) vs.\ which are set-and-forget?
(3) Is there a safety default: ``if in doubt, $p{=}1$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$ at the two-phase default, Hill $k{=}0.1n$'' -- so the hyperparameter section is usable without reading the appendix?}

\paragraph{When the correction term is unused.}
When the latent dimension \(m\) exceeds the intrinsic dimension of the data manifold, the homogeneous component \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\) has spare angular capacity and can absorb moderate departures from conical geometry without the correction \(\delta\) being active; this has been observed empirically on manifolds whose intrinsic dimension is unknown or non-integer. The correction term becomes essential only when \(m\) is tight against the manifold dimension and the data carries persistent curvature.

\paragraph{Tail-aware reconstruction loss.}
A natural extension of \S\ref{sec:training-objective} is to weight the reconstruction term itself by an increasing function of the ambient radius, encouraging the encoder to match tail samples more accurately at the cost of a potential bulk-reconstruction trade-off. We leave a systematic study to future work.

\subsection{Proof of
\texorpdfstring{\Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}}{Proposition~\ref*{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}}
(encoder tail transport)}
\label{sec:appendix:proof-encoder-tail-transport}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}]
Define
\[
\nu_t(\cdot)
:=
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)}\in \cdot\right).
\]
By assumption that \(X\) is regularly varying, \(\nu_t \to \mu\) in \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D)\). Since \(f\) satisfies the hypotheses of the mapping theorem (\Cref{thm:mapping-theorem}; see \cite{lindskog_regularly_2014}, Theorem~2.3 and \cite[Prop.~3.12]{Resnick_2007}) and since \(f\) is continuous \(\mu\)-almost everywhere, so that \(\mu(D_f)=0\), it follows that
\[
\nu_t \circ f^{-1}
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
\mu\circ f^{-1}
=
\mu_f
\qquad
\text{in } \mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_m).
\]
Now, for any measurable \(A\subset\mathbb{O}_m\) bounded away from \(0\), we have
\[
(\nu_t\circ f^{-1})(A)
=
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)} \in f^{-1}(A)\right)
=
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(f\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)}\right)\in A\right).
\]
Then, by \(p\)-homogeneity of \(f\),
\[
f\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)}\right)
=
\frac{f(X)}{b(t)^p},
\]
so
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{f(X)}{b(t)^p}\in A\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
\mu_f(A).
\]
Therefore \(f(X)\) is regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) with scaling function \(b_f(t)=b(t)^p\) and tail measure \(\mu_f=f_{\#}\mu = \mu\circ f^{-1}\).

To identify the tail (homogeneity) index of \(\mu_f\), let \(A\subset\mathbb{O}_m\) be measurable and bounded away from \(0\) and let \(\lambda>0\). By homogeneity of \(f\), we have \(f(x)\in\lambda A\) if and only if \(f(\lambda^{-1/p}x)\in A\), which gives
\[
f^{-1}(\lambda A)=\lambda^{1/p} f^{-1}(A).
\]
Therefore,
\[
\mu_f(\lambda A)
=
\mu\bigl(f^{-1}(\lambda A)\bigr)
=
\mu\bigl(\lambda^{1/p} f^{-1}(A)\bigr)
=
\lambda^{-\frac{\alpha}{p}}\mu\bigl(f^{-1}(A)\bigr)
=
\lambda^{-\frac{\alpha}{p}}\mu_f(A).
\]
Thus \(\mu_f\) is \(\frac{\alpha}{p}\)-homogeneous. Equivalently, \(f(X)\) is regularly varying with tail index \(\frac{\alpha}{p}\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of
\texorpdfstring{\Cref{lem:uniform-angular-convergence}}{Lemma~\ref*{lem:uniform-angular-convergence}}
(uniform angular convergence)}
\label{sec:appendix:proof-uniform-angular-convergence}
\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{lem:uniform-angular-convergence}]
The set \(\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q\times[0,1]\) is compact. Since \(\Delta\) is
continuous on this compact set, it is uniformly continuous. Hence, for every
\(\varepsilon>0\), there exists \(s_0>0\) such that
\[
0\le s<s_0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q}
\|\Delta(\eta,s)-\Delta(\eta,0)\|_q<\varepsilon.
\]
Since \(s(\rho)=1/(1+\rho)\to0\), this gives
\[
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q}
\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_q
=
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q}
\|\Delta(\eta,s(\rho))-\Delta(\eta,0)\|_q
\to0.
\]

Set
\[
c_0:=
\inf_{\eta,\rho}
\|\tilde e(\eta)+\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_q>0.
\]
For \(u,v\neq0\), the normalisation map \(N(w)=w/\|w\|_q\) satisfies, whenever
\(\|u\|_q\ge c_0\) and \(\|v\|_q=1\),
\[
\left\|\frac{u}{\|u\|_q}-v\right\|_q
\le
\frac{2}{c_0}\|u-v\|_q.
\]
Applying this with \(u=\tilde e(\eta)+\delta(\eta,\rho)\) and
\(v=\tilde e(\eta)\) yields
\[
\|\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\|_q
\le
\frac{2}{c_0}\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_q.
\]
Taking the supremum over \(\eta\) and sending \(\rho\to\infty\) proves
\[
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q}
\|\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\|_q
\to0.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of
\texorpdfstring{\Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence}}{Theorem~\ref*{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence}}
(decoder tail equivalence)}
\label{sec:appendix:proof-decoder-tail-equivalence}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence}]
Write \(Z=\rho\eta\), where \(\rho=\|Z\|_q\) and
\(\eta=Z/\|Z\|_q\). Define
\[
\varepsilon(\rho)
:=
\sup_{\eta\in\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q}
\|\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\|_q,
\qquad
\varepsilon(\rho)\to0.
\]

\textbf{Step 1: regular variation of the homogeneous leading term.}
The map
\[
h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)
=
\|z\|_q^{1/p}\,
\tilde a\!\left(\frac{z}{\|z\|_q}\right)
\tilde e\!\left(\frac{z}{\|z\|_q}\right)
\]
is continuous on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) by (A1)--(A2), positively homogeneous of
degree \(1/p\), and satisfies
\[
a_{\min}\|z\|_q^{1/p}
\le
\|h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)\|_q
\le
a_{\max}\|z\|_q^{1/p}.
\]
Hence preimages under \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\) of sets bounded away from zero are
bounded away from zero. The mapping theorem applied to the regularly varying
random vector \(Z\) gives
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(
\frac{h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}\in\cdot
\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
(h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z(\cdot)
\qquad
\text{in }\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D).
\]

\textbf{Step 2: deterministic perturbation bound.}
For every \(z=\rho\eta\in\mathbb{O}_m\),
\[
h(z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)
=
\rho^{1/p}\tilde a(\eta)\bigl(\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\bigr).
\]
Therefore
\[
\|h(z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)\|_q
\le
a_{\max}\rho^{1/p}\varepsilon(\rho).
\]

\textbf{Step 3: negligibility after tail normalisation.}
We show that, for every \(\gamma>0\),
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(
\frac{\|h(Z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\|_q}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}>\gamma
\right)
\longrightarrow0.
\]
Fix \(\gamma>0\) and \(\delta>0\). By regular variation of \(Z\), the radial
tail satisfies
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{\|Z\|_q}{b_Z(t)}>M\right)
\longrightarrow
\mu_Z\bigl(\{z:\|z\|_q>M\}\bigr)
\]
at all continuity radii \(M\) of the limiting radial measure. Since
\(\mu_Z\) is homogeneous and finite on sets bounded away from the origin,
we may choose such an \(M>1\) so that
\[
\limsup_{t\to\infty}
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{\|Z\|_q}{b_Z(t)}>M\right)
<\delta.
\]
Since \(\varepsilon(\rho)\to0\), choose \(R>0\) such that
\[
a_{\max}M^{1/p}\varepsilon(\rho)\le\gamma
\qquad
\text{for all }\rho\ge R.
\]
For all sufficiently large \(t\), \(R\le M b_Z(t)\). On the event
\[
R\le \|Z\|_q\le M b_Z(t),
\]
the perturbation estimate from Step~2 gives
\[
\frac{\|h(Z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\|_q}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}
\le
a_{\max}
\left(\frac{\|Z\|_q}{b_Z(t)}\right)^{1/p}
\varepsilon(\|Z\|_q)
\le
a_{\max}M^{1/p}\varepsilon(\|Z\|_q)
\le
\gamma.
\]
On the complementary small-radius event \(\{\|Z\|_q<R\}\), we use only the
boundedness of the angular discrepancy. Since
\[
\|\tilde c(\eta,\rho)-\tilde e(\eta)\|_q\le 2
\]
for unit \(q\)-norm directions, Step~2 implies
\[
\|h(Z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\|_q
\le
2a_{\max}R^{1/p}
\qquad
\text{on }\{\|Z\|_q<R\}.
\]
Because \(b_Z(t)^{1/p}\to\infty\), the event
\[
\left\{
\|Z\|_q<R,\,
\frac{\|h(Z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\|_q}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}>\gamma
\right\}
\]
is empty for all sufficiently large \(t\). Therefore, for all sufficiently
large \(t\),
\[
\left\{
\frac{\|h(Z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\|_q}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}>\gamma
\right\}
\subseteq
\left\{\frac{\|Z\|_q}{b_Z(t)}>M\right\}.
\]
It follows that
\[
\limsup_{t\to\infty}
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(
\frac{\|h(Z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\|_q}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}>\gamma
\right)
\le \delta.
\]
Since \(\delta>0\) was arbitrary, the required negligibility follows.

\textbf{Step 4: converging together in \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D)\).}
Step~1 gives the \(\mathbb{M}\)-limit of the homogeneous leading term, and
Step~3 shows that the difference between \(h(Z)\) and \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\)
is negligible at the same tail scale. The standard converging-together
argument for regularly varying random vectors therefore yields
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(
\frac{h(Z)}{b_Z(t)^{1/p}}\in\cdot
\right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
(h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z(\cdot)
\qquad
\text{in }\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D).
\]
Thus \(h(Z)\) and \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}(Z)\) are tail-equivalent with common
scaling function \(b_Z^{1/p}\) and common tail measure
\((h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Rate under Lipschitz angular correction]
If \(\Delta\) is Lipschitz in \(s\) near \(s=0\), uniformly in \(\eta\), then
the compact-domain construction
\[
\delta(\eta,\rho)
=
\Delta(\eta,s(\rho))-\Delta(\eta,0),
\qquad
s(\rho)=\frac{1}{1+\rho},
\]
implies
\[
\sup_{\eta}\|\delta(\eta,\rho)\|_q
=
O(\rho^{-1}).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\|h(z)-h_{\mathrm{hom}}(z)\|_q
=
O(\rho^{r-1})
\qquad
(\rho=\|z\|_q\to\infty).
\]
In the encoder--decoder setting \(r=1/p\). In particular, for the default
choice \(p=1\), the unnormalised decoder perturbation is bounded in ambient
norm, while its contribution at the tail scale is still negligible.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Proof of
\texorpdfstring{\Cref{cor:tail-index-preservation}}{Corollary~\ref*{cor:tail-index-preservation}}
(round-trip tail-index preservation)}
\label{sec:appendix:proof-tail-index-preservation}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{cor:tail-index-preservation}]
By \Cref{prop:reg-variation-under-homogeneous-encoders}, \(Z=f(X)\) is regularly varying on \(\mathbb{O}_m\) with scaling function \(b_Z(t)=b_X(t)^p\), tail index \(\alpha/p\), and tail measure \(\mu_Z=f_{\#}\mu_X\). Applying \Cref{thm:decoder-tail-equivalence} with \(r=1/p\) gives that \(h(Z)\) is regularly varying with scaling function
\[
b_Z(t)^{1/p}=\bigl(b_X(t)^p\bigr)^{1/p}=b_X(t)
\]
and tail measure
\[
(h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}\mu_Z=(h_{\mathrm{hom}})_{\#}f_{\#}\mu_X=(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X.
\]
The homogeneity degree of the limiting measure is therefore \(-\alpha\), so the round-trip tail index is \(\alpha\).
\end{proof}

\section{Extended theoretical discussion}

\subsection{Radial--angular factorisation of homogeneous maps}
\label{sec:appendix:canonical-form}

In this section we show that every positively homogeneous map on
\(\mathbb{R}^D\setminus\{0\}\) admits a unique radial--angular factorisation
for a given norm. This is useful in a machine learning setting: since homogeneity is enforced by construction in this representation, one may parameterise the angular component unrestricted while always satisfying the required scaling law. This observation motivates the encoder architecture introduced later in
Section~\ref{sec:encoder-architecture}.

Let
\[
\mathbb{O}_D := \mathbb{R}^D \setminus \{0\}.
\]
Fix \(q \in [1,\infty]\). For each \(x \in \mathbb{O}_D\), write
\[
x = \rho\,\theta,
\qquad
\rho := \|x\|_{q} > 0,
\qquad
\theta := \frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}} \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q},
\]
where
\[
\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q} := \{u \in \mathbb{R}^D : \|u\|_{q} = 1\}.
\]

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{prop:canonical-homogeneous-form}]
Firstly, to verify that the provided factorisation is indeed homogeneous, suppose that
\[
f(x) = \|x\|_{q}^p\, g\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right),
\qquad x \in \mathbb{O}_D.
\]
Then for every \(\lambda > 0\),
\[
f(\lambda x)
=
\|\lambda x\|_{q}^p\, g\!\left(\frac{\lambda x}{\|\lambda x\|_{q}}\right)
=
(\lambda \|x\|_{q})^p\, g\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right)
=
\lambda^p f(x),
\]
since \(\|\lambda x\|_{q} = \lambda \|x\|_{q}\) for \(\lambda > 0\). Thus \(f\) is positively homogeneous of degree \(p\).

Now to show that any valid f always admits a radial--angular factorisation, assume that \(f\) is positively homogeneous of degree \(p\). Define
\[
g(\theta) := f(\theta),
\qquad \theta \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q}.
\]
For any \(x \in \mathbb{O}_D\), we have
\[
x = \|x\|_{q} \frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}},
\]
and therefore, by positive homogeneity,
\[
f(x)
=
f\!\left(\|x\|_{q} \frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right)
=
\|x\|_{q}^p\, f\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right)
=
\|x\|_{q}^p\, g\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right).
\]
This proves existence.

To prove uniqueness, suppose also that
\[
f(x) = \|x\|_{q}^p\, \tilde g\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right)
\]
for some map \(\tilde g : \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q} \to \mathbb{R}^m\). Let
\(\theta \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q}\). Since \(\|\theta\|_{q} = 1\), evaluating both
representations at \(x=\theta\) gives
\[
f(\theta)=g(\theta)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
f(\theta)=\tilde g(\theta).
\]
Therefore
\[
g(\theta)=\tilde g(\theta)
\qquad
\text{for every } \theta \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q}.
\]
Hence \(g=\tilde g\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\Cref{prop:canonical-homogeneous-form} shows that, for a given norm
\(\|\cdot\|_{q}\), a positively homogeneous map is completely determined by its
restriction to the \(q\)-sphere: the scaling behaviour is prescribed by \(\|x\|_{q}^p\), and all remaining structure is represented by the angular component \(g\).
\end{remark}

A corollary of \ref{prop:canonical-homogeneous-form} is that, on the subset
\[
\{\theta \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q} : g(\theta) \neq 0\},
\]
one may further decompose \(g\) into magnitude and direction:
\[
g(\theta) = a(\theta)e(\theta),
\qquad
a(\theta) := \|g(\theta)\|_{q},
\qquad
e(\theta) := \frac{g(\theta)}{\|g(\theta)\|_{q}} \in \mathbb{S}^{m-1}_{q},
\]
where \(q \in [1,\infty]\) is a latent-space norm exponent. For notational simplicity, we use \(q\) to denote norm exponents in both the ambient space \(\mathbb{R}^D\) and the latent space \(\mathbb{R}^m\). However, these exponents need not be equal in practice, allowing flexibility in the choice of norm for each space.

Thus, for every \(x \in \mathbb{O}_D\) such that
\(g(x/\|x\|_{q}) \neq 0\), we always have the decomposition
\[
f(x)
=
\|x\|_{q}^p\,
a\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right)
e\!\left(\frac{x}{\|x\|_{q}}\right).
\]

Under this representation, \(a\) can be considered as an angle-dependent scaling factor, \(e\) as an angle-map, and \(\|x\|_{q}^p\) as a purely radial scaling term.

\begin{remark}[Exact vs.\ bounded radial preservation]
\label{rmk:exact-vs-bounded-radial}
The angular magnitude \(a(\theta)\) exposes a three-tier trade-off between expressiveness and interpretability of the latent tail measure \(\mu_f=f_{\#}\mu\):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(i)] \(a(\theta)\equiv 1\): the encoder is a radial isometry, \(\|f(x)\|_q=\|x\|_q^{p}\), so latent radius is a deterministic function of ambient radius and \(\mu_f=e_{\#}(\mu|_{\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_q})\); only data whose tail measure concentrates on an embedded sphere can be reconstructed.
  \item[(ii)] \(0<a_{\min}\le a(\theta)\le a_{\max}<\infty\) (bounded variant): the latent tail index is unchanged and \(\mu_f\) is the pushforward of \(\mu\) modulo a bounded angular reweighting by \(a(\theta)\); this covers cases where data concentrates near a cone with nonuniform angular density.
  \item[(iii)] \(a(\theta)\) unrestricted positive (our default): \(\mu_f\) is a general pushforward, maximising expressiveness at the cost of a closed-form geometric interpretation.
\end{enumerate}
Our implementation uses tier~(iii) via \(a(\theta)=\mathrm{softplus}(A_\phi(\theta))\) (\Cref{sec:appendix:architecture}); tier~(ii) is recovered by clamping the softplus output to \([a_{\min},a_{\max}]\).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Universal approximation of the corrected angular decoder]
\label{rmk:universal-approx-delta}
For any continuous target map \(c^{*}:\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q\times[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}^D\) and any \(\varepsilon>0\), the universal-approximation theorem for continuous MLPs on compact sets~\todo{CITE Hornik 1991 / Cybenko 1989 / Pinkus 1999} supplies a network \(\Delta\) with \(\sup_{(\eta,s)}\|\Delta(\eta,s)-c^{*}(\eta,s)\|<\varepsilon\); the subtraction \(\delta(\eta,\rho)=\Delta(\eta,s(\rho))-\Delta(\eta,0)\) preserves approximation quality up to a doubling of the error, so \(\tilde c(\eta,\rho)\) can in principle approximate any continuous corrected angular decoder uniformly on \(\mathbb{S}^{m-1}_q\times[0,1]\). This density result ensures \Cref{lem:uniform-angular-convergence} does not curtail architectural expressiveness.
\end{remark}

More generally, the same argument applies whenever one replaces
\(\|x\|_{q}\) by a positive radial function \(r : \mathbb{O}_D \to (0,\infty)\)
satisfying
\[
r(\lambda x) = \lambda r(x),
\qquad x \in \mathbb{O}_D,\ \lambda > 0,
\]
and uses an associated normalisation \(x \mapsto x/r(x)\).

\subsection{Tail-index versus full tail-measure recovery}
\label{sec:appendix:index-vs-measure}

\Cref{cor:tail-index-preservation} preserves the radial tail index and gives an explicit reconstructed tail measure \((h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X\). It does not, however, imply
\[
(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f)_{\#}\mu_X=\mu_X.
\]
Full ambient tail-measure recovery would require the homogeneous round-trip \(h_{\mathrm{hom}}\circ f\) to preserve the original tail measure --- an additional asymptotic inverse condition, not a consequence of homogeneity alone. Latent-space extreme-value analysis is therefore calibrated at the level of the global radial tail index by construction; the reconstructed process has a computable tail measure obtained by homogeneous pushforward, and applications requiring exact angular tail-measure recovery must either impose or empirically verify the stronger condition.

\paragraph{Encoder parameterisation.}

a(\theta)=\mathrm{softplus}\!\bigl(A_\phi(\theta)\bigr),
\qquad

\[
A_\phi:\mathbb{S}^{D-1}_{q}\to\mathbb{R},
\qquad

\section{Training details}
\label{sec:appendix:training}

\paragraph{Implementation choices.}
In our experiments the following implementation choices are used: $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}}$ is the mean-squared error, $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{pen}} = \|\cdot\|_2$, and the latent-space norm exponent is $q=2$.

\paragraph{Adaptive $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$ schedule.}
Rather than treating $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}}$ as a fixed hyperparameter, we employ an adaptive schedule that prioritises reconstruction fidelity before gradually incentivising homogeneity.  Training occurs in two phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Phase A (warmup).} The model trains with $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}} = 0$ (pure reconstruction) until the validation reconstruction loss has converged, operationalised as no improvement in the best validation reconstruction for $N_{\mathrm{recon}}$ consecutive epochs.  A hard cap $T_{\mathrm{warmup-max}}$ on the number of warmup epochs guarantees the phase terminates on pathological runs.  At exit we record an exponential moving average (EMA) baseline~$b$ of the validation reconstruction loss.
    \item \emph{Phase B (growth/decay).} At each subsequent epoch the EMA of the validation reconstruction loss is compared against the tolerance $b(1 + \tau)$.  If the current EMA does not exceed this threshold, $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}} \leftarrow \min(\lambda_{\max},\, \lambda_{\mathrm{cor}} \cdot g)$; otherwise $\lambda_{\mathrm{cor}} \leftarrow \lambda_{\mathrm{cor}} \cdot d$, with $d < 1$.
\end{enumerate}
The aim of this is to satisfy reconstruction first, then enforce homogeneity to the extent that the network can absorb it without degrading reconstruction quality.  In our synthetic experiments we set $N_{\mathrm{recon}} = 100$, $T_{\mathrm{warmup-max}} = 1000$, $g = 1.10$, $d = 0.95$, $\tau = 0.50$, and $\lambda_{\max} = 1.0$.  We additionally apply gradient norm clipping with a maximum norm of~$1.0$, Phase-B early stopping on the full validation loss with patience $N_{\mathrm{penalty}} = 600$, and retain the best-validation checkpoint.  The standard (non-homogeneous) autoencoder baseline has no penalty phase and uses $N_{\mathrm{recon}}$ as its sole early-stopping budget; its hidden-layer width is chosen so that the total parameter count matches that of the homogeneous model.

\subsection{Regular variation: formal definitions}
\label{sec:appendix:metrics:rv}

(Moved from body \S\ref{sec:prelim:rv} per P-3.  Body keeps a compressed
4--6 line pointer; the full exposition lives here.)

We consider a scenario in which the ambient data is heavy-tailed (in the sense of regular variation). The regime of high-dimensional, heavy-tailed and likely manifold-supported observations is a particularly common challenge in several fields, such as meteorology, astronomy and finance. To state this precisely, define the punctured spaces
\[
\mathbb{O}_D := \mathbb{R}^D \setminus \{0\},
\qquad
\mathbb{O}_m := \mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{0\}.
\]
Throughout this work, we assume \(\mathbb{P}(X = 0) = 0\), so that \(X\) may be regarded as an \(\mathbb{O}_D\)-valued random vector almost surely. Let \(\nu = \mathcal{L}(X)\) denote the law of \(X\) on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) and \(\mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D)\) the space of measures on \(\mathbb{O}_D\). Following \cite{lindskog_regularly_2014}, we say that \(X\) is \emph{regularly varying} on \(\mathbb{O}_D\) with index \(\alpha > 0\) if there exists an increasing scaling function
\[
b:(0,\infty)\to(0,\infty),
\qquad
b(t)\to\infty \quad \text{as } t\to\infty,
\]
and a nonzero measure \(\mu \in \mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D)\) such that
\[
t\,\nu\bigl(b(t)\,\cdot\bigr)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
\mu(\cdot)
\qquad
\text{in } \mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D).
\]
Equivalently,
\[
t\,\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{X}{b(t)} \in \cdot \right)
\;\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}\;
\mu(\cdot)
\qquad
\text{in } \mathbb{M}(\mathbb{O}_D).
\]
The measure \(\mu\) is referred to as the \emph{ambient tail measure} of \(X\). By \cite[Theorem 3.1]{lindskog_regularly_2014}, the limit measure \(\mu\) is \(\alpha\)-homogeneous:
\[
\mu(\lambda A)=\lambda^{-\alpha}\mu(A),
\qquad
\lambda>0,
\]
for all measurable \(A \subset \mathbb{O}_D\). See also \cite[Theorem 6.1 and Section 6.5.5]{resnick_heavy-tail_2007}. For avoidance of confusion, note that here and throughout, homogeneity of \emph{measures} refers to scaling of dilated sets: \(\mu(\lambda A)=\lambda^{-\alpha}\mu(A)\), whereas homogeneity of \emph{maps} refers to the usual scaling relation \(f(\lambda x)= \lambda^p f(x)\).
